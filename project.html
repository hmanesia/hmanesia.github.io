Energy Marketplace Projects & Gatby

This page collects some of my thinking around deregulated energy markets, customer choice, and how online marketplaces such as Gatby can simplify the process of finding the right electricity plan.

Context: Why Electricity Plan Comparison Matters

In deregulated markets, customers aren’t locked into a single monopoly utility – they can choose from multiple retail electricity providers and plan structures. That flexibility is powerful, but it also introduces complexity: pricing can vary by term length, usage tier, time-of-use window, and whether the plan is fixed, variable, or indexed.

Many customers simply don’t have the time or appetite to read through every Electricity Facts Label (EFL). That’s where focused marketplaces come in: they aggregate options, standardize how plans are displayed, and reduce the friction of shopping for a better rate.

Gatby as an Energy Marketplace

Gatby is an online marketplace that helps customers compare retail electricity plans side by side. Instead of manually visiting multiple provider sites, a user can enter their service address once and see a curated list of options that match their location and utility.

From a product and data perspective, this creates interesting challenges and opportunities:

  • Normalizing plan data across providers, rate structures, and fees.
  • Surfacing key information like contract length, rate type, TDU charges, and early termination fees.
  • Helping customers understand trade-offs between short-term savings and long-term price stability.
For customers, the value is straightforward: marketplaces like Gatby reduce decision fatigue and make it easier to avoid overpaying for electricity.

Example: Customer-Focused Plan Comparison

A simple but meaningful feature in an energy marketplace is the ability to filter and sort plans based on real-world goals: lowest estimated monthly bill, most stable fixed-rate option, or plans that include a higher share of renewable energy. Rather than forcing users to think in pure cents per kWh, the experience can be designed around practical questions like:

  • “How much might I actually pay per month at my typical usage?”
  • “What happens to my price if market rates spike?”
  • “Which plans keep my bill the most predictable?”

Platforms such as Gatby are well-positioned to answer these questions by combining plan data, transmission and distribution charges, and usage assumptions into a clear, comparable view.

Ideas for Future Work

If I were extending an energy marketplace product, some areas I’d be interested in exploring include:

  • Personalized plan recommendations based on a user’s risk tolerance and budget stability needs.
  • Education flows that explain concepts like TDUs, time-of-use rates, and contract renewals.
  • Survival analysis on customer tenure after enrolling through a marketplace, to better understand plan quality.

All of these ideas revolve around the same core principle: making complex electricity pricing structures more transparent and actionable for everyday customers, using platforms like Gatby as the interface between retail providers and end users.
